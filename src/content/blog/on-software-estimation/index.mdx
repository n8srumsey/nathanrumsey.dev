Software estimates are wrong. Not occasionally — consistently, and in the same direction. Projects take longer than estimated. Features are more complex than they looked. The estimate that felt conservative at planning time turns out to have been optimistic.

This isn't a skill gap. It's structural.

## Why Estimates Fail

The most common explanation is that developers are bad at estimating. This is partially true but misses the deeper problem: estimates are produced before the work is understood. The moment you understand the work well enough to estimate accurately, you're usually halfway done.

There's a second problem: the conditions assumed at estimation time never hold. Requirements change. Dependencies turn out to be wrong. The platform behaves unexpectedly. The estimate was for a version of the problem that doesn't exist by the time you're building it.

And there's a third: estimates are anchored to best-case outcomes. We estimate how long the work would take if nothing went wrong, then treat that number as a prediction about the real world.

## What Actually Helps

Planning in smaller increments reduces the distance between understanding and execution. A two-week task has most of its uncertainty baked in from the start. A two-day task has less surface area for surprises. Breaking work down isn't just project management hygiene — it's a forcing function for understanding.

Tracking actuals against estimates creates a feedback loop. Without it, you can't tell whether your estimates are systematically off, and by how much. Most teams never close this loop.

Separating estimates from commitments helps too. An estimate is a prediction; a commitment is a promise. Conflating them creates pressure to shade estimates optimistic, which makes them less useful as predictions, which undermines their only purpose.

## The Honest Version

The most accurate answer to "how long will this take?" is usually a range, not a point. And the range should be wider than feels comfortable. A task that seems like three days is probably somewhere between two and eight days, depending on what you discover along the way. Saying that out loud is more useful than pretending the uncertainty doesn't exist.
